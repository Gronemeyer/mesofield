# Summary

Linking brain activity to behavior increasingly requires recording many signals at once — cortical fluorescence, pupil diameter, facial and body movement, and locomotion — from the same animal, and doing so repeatedly across weeks. The hardware that makes this possible is, by necessity, bespoke, and the acquisition code that drives it tends to be rig-specific, weakly documented, and difficult to reproduce. We present Mesofield, an open-source Python framework that orchestrates multi-modal hardware acquisition and emits analysis-ready output organized to the Brain Imaging Data Structure (BIDS) and described by a machine- and human-readable acquisition manifest. A single `Procedure` object owns the experiment configuration and hardware lifecycle; the same code path runs whether an experiment is launched from a graphical interface for real-time visualization or executed headlessly as a script, which keeps interactive prototyping and batch acquisition reproducible against one another. Devices communicate over a uniform signal bus, so heterogeneous backends — a MicroManager-controlled mesoscope, an OpenCV camera, a serial encoder — are collected identically. New devices are added through a small registration contract, and a downstream toolkit reads the manifest rather than guessing at filenames. Mesofield is intended as a shareable substrate for reproducible, modality-agnostic acquisition in widefield mesoscale neuroscience.

**Keywords:** widefield imaging; mesoscale cortical imaging; multi-modal acquisition; reproducibility; BIDS; data provenance; MicroManager; open-source software; neurophysiology

# Introduction

Understanding how brain states give rise to behavior requires measuring more than one thing at a time. A modern systems-neuroscience experiment routinely pairs a measure of neural activity with pupillometry, facial and whisker movement, heart and respiratory rate, and locomotor activity on a wheel or treadmill, because the variable being explained — cortical dynamics — turns out to be substantially explained by the very behaviors recorded alongside it. Cortex-wide activity during a trained task is dominated by movement, much of it spontaneous and uninstructed rather than task-related [@musall2019movements], and the same high-dimensional behavioral signal is legible across the forebrain even in the absence of an explicit task [@stringer2019spontaneous]. The practical consequence is unavoidable: if the movements are not captured at acquisition time, on the same clock as the neural signal, the dominant source of variance in the neural data is simply lost. Multi-modal capture is therefore not an enrichment but a prerequisite for interpretation, and it places demands on acquisition tooling that span electrical engineering, real-time software, and control systems.

Widefield tandem-lens imaging of the mouse dorsal cortex has become a workhorse for measuring activity at the mesoscale, where coordinated fluctuations of local neuronal populations can be read out across most of the cortical surface at once [@rynes2021minimscope]. The approach pairs a genetically encoded calcium indicator — increasingly a fast, sensitive GCaMP variant such as jGCaMP8 [@zhang2023jgcamp8] — with a macroscope built from off-the-shelf photographic lenses. Detailed protocols exist for assembling such macroscopes, preparing the cranial window, and running chronic, cortex-wide sessions in behaving mice [@couto2021chronic]. {Describe the hardware/widefield setup under the MicroManager guise — to be expanded with the specific optical path, sensor, and illumination used on the Sipe Lab rig.} What these protocols deliberately do not standardize is the acquisition software itself: how the cameras, encoders, microcontrollers, and stimulus computers in a given rig are coordinated, and — more consequentially — what comes off disk when a session ends.

That gap matters because longitudinal, multi-modal designs generate large and heterogeneous data. Acquisition devices within and across setups produce idiosyncratic outputs at different stages: raw image stacks, CSV tables, raw serial bytes, or proprietary binary formats, only some of which are later consolidated into an analysis-ready matrix. As Griffin and colleagues put it, "scientific data must be thought of in the context of the entire data lifecycle, which spans planning, acquisition, processing, and analysis to publication and reuse" [@griffin2017lifecycle]. The reusability end of that lifecycle has been codified into community standards — the FAIR principles for findable, accessible, interoperable, and reusable data [@wilkinson2016fair], the Brain Imaging Data Structure for organizing neuroimaging outputs [@gorgolewski2016bids], and Neurodata Without Borders for neurophysiology [@rubel2022nwb] — but standards describe a destination, not the route. The planning and acquisition end, where data provenance is actually established or lost, remains largely the province of bespoke per-lab code.

The failure mode is familiar to anyone who has set up a rig. Acquisition code is written for one hardware configuration, accumulates undocumented assumptions, and produces outputs whose generating parameters are not recorded alongside them — so that months later the question "what settings produced this file?" has no reliable answer. These are the {growing pains} a lab feels most acutely when standing up a new system or onboarding a trainee, and they are worst during pilot experiments, precisely when parameters change fastest and documentation discipline is weakest.

Existing software addresses adjacent problems without closing this one. MicroManager provides mature, cross-platform control of microscope hardware [@edelstein2014micromanager], and Pycro-Manager exposes that control to Python for customized, reproducible automation [@pinkard2021pycromanager]; both are device-control layers rather than experiment frameworks, and neither prescribes a multi-modal output contract. ACQ4 offers an integrated acquisition-and-analysis platform oriented toward patch-clamp and photostimulation electrophysiology [@campagnola2014acq4], and ImSwitch generalizes microscope control with a modular Python architecture [@casasmoreno2021imswitch]; tools such as PyZebrascope and lab-specific stacks like Meso-Py serve their respective modalities. What is missing for multi-modal mesoscale work is a thin orchestration layer that treats the rig configuration as a versioned, reproducible artifact and binds it to a documented output contract — {reproducible config, contract-bound outputs} — while remaining agnostic about which modalities are present.

Mesofield is our attempt to fill that gap. It is a configurable Python framework that orchestrates multi-modal hardware acquisition and produces BIDS-organized output described by a structured acquisition manifest, runnable either through a graphical interface for real-time visualization or as a headless script for batch and scripted use. We are deliberate about what Mesofield claims and what it does not. It is not a device-driver project, a replacement for MicroManager, or a production-hardened turnkey product; it presupposes some familiarity with the underlying hardware and with MicroManager device configuration, and it has been exercised primarily on Windows. What it does claim is to make the configuration of a multi-modal acquisition explicit, reproducible, and self-documenting, and to make the resulting data legible to downstream tools without bespoke parsing. The sections that follow describe the architecture that delivers those properties (Figure 1), a representative multi-modal longitudinal acquisition (Figure 2), the device-extension contract (Figure 3), and the acquisition-to-analysis ecosystem the manifest enables (Figure 4).

# Results

## Architecture and design (Figure 1)

Mesofield's architecture is built around a single orchestrator so that the path from "press start" to "data on disk" passes through one auditable place. An experimental session is driven by a `Procedure` object that contains zero device-specific logic. The `Procedure` owns an `ExperimentConfig`, which in turn owns a `HardwareManager` — three nested responsibilities that separate *what is being run* (subject, session, task, runtime variables) from *what it is being run on* (the cameras, encoders, and microcontrollers attached to the computer). Device-specific behavior lives in subclasses that live outside the package, typically under `experiments/<name>/procedure.py`, and are discovered at launch from fields in the experiment configuration. The base `Procedure` exposes a small set of subclass hooks — `prerun`, `on_started`, `on_finished` — around a fixed lifecycle: bring hardware up, arm every device, wire the termination signal, start every device, then save and clean up. The effect is that an experimenter writes only the logic unique to their paradigm and inherits the orchestration, error handling, and output contract unchanged.

Two properties of this design do the reproducibility work. The first is that there is exactly one set of code paths, reachable two ways. An experiment can be launched from the graphical interface (`mesofield launch`) for interactive setup and real-time data visualization, or executed headlessly (`python procedure.py`) for scripted and batch runs; both entry points construct and drive the same `Procedure` through the same lifecycle. The GUI is a view onto the orchestrator, not a parallel implementation of it, so a result obtained interactively and one obtained in a script are reproductions of each other rather than two code paths that happen to agree. This is the kind of guarantee that is easy to assert and easy to lose; keeping it true is a continuing design constraint rather than a finished achievement.

The second property is a uniform signal bus. Every hardware device exposes a `DeviceSignals` bundle with four standard signals — `started`, `finished`, `data`, and `frame` — built on the `psygnal` library so that emission is Qt-free, weakly referenced, and thread-safe. Devices emit on this bus; the `DataManager` and its thread-safe `DataQueue` collect from it uniformly, regardless of whether the datum originated from a MicroManager-controlled camera, an OpenCV capture, or a serial encoder. A camera-like producer additionally emits raw frames on the optional `frame` signal for real-time processing, while producers without per-sample raw payloads simply never emit it. Because the collection layer subscribes to an interface rather than to concrete device types, adding a backend does not require touching the collection layer — a separation that becomes important for extensibility (Figure 3). When GUI code needs a Qt slot, a small bridge forwards a `psygnal` emission to a `pyqtSignal`, keeping the Qt dependency confined to the interface.

Configuration is treated as a first-class, versioned artifact. The `HardwareManager` is materialized from a YAML rig file; the `ExperimentConfig` carries subject, session, task, and arbitrary user-defined metadata and lays out a BIDS-style directory hierarchy (`experiment_dir` → `data_dir` → `bids_dir`). The rig file is the anchor, and experiment parameters are optional on top of it, which means a hardware configuration can exist and be launched on its own with parameters authored later in the GUI. Both the hardware and experiment configurations are written to disk alongside the data they produced, so that the question of which parameters generated a given session has a file-level answer rather than a remembered one. **Figure 1** depicts this as three nested boxes — `Procedure` → `ExperimentConfig` → `HardwareManager` — connected to a horizontal signal bus from which the `DataManager`/`DataQueue` collect; class-level detail is deferred to STAR Methods.

## Multi-modal, longitudinal acquisition (Figure 2)

To show the framework carrying a real workload, Figure 2 walks through a representative session on a widefield rig: a tandem-lens mesoscope imaging GCaMP8s over the dorsal cortex, a camera trained on the eye for pupillometry, and a wheel or treadmill encoder reporting locomotion, run under the [HFSA paradigm — to be specified]. The concrete acquisition results and timing traces for this figure are deferred pending the application dataset; what follows describes the mechanism the figure will instantiate.

The central technical problem in multi-modal acquisition is shared time. Mesofield designates one device as the primary by setting `primary: true` in the rig YAML; with a single device the role is assigned automatically, so simple setups need not declare it. The primary device anchors session-level timing, and its `finished` signal is what triggers the framework's cleanup — whichever terminates the run, the primary's completion or an explicit duration cap, the other is disconnected cleanly so that non-primary capture threads do not hang. For hardware-level alignment, the custom MicroManager engines can pulse an NI-DAQ line at sequence start, giving downstream instruments a physical synchronization edge rather than relying on software timestamps alone. Each producer records its own device-side timestamps under a declared `TimeBasis` (for example, a wall-clock Unix-seconds clock source), and the `DataQueue` records arrival order, so that modalities can be related on a common basis at analysis time. We are intentionally modest here: hardware synchronization quality is a property to be measured, not assumed, and quantifying it across modalities is part of the Performance and Reliability characterization (below, deferred).

The longitudinal dimension falls out of the configuration split. Across N sessions of the same subject, the rig file stays fixed — the hardware identity, device parameters, and synchronization wiring are the constant — while the per-session experiment parameters (session number, task, subject-specific variables) are re-resolved each run. Reusing one immutable hardware specification across many sessions, and conversely running different experiments against one specification, is what makes within-lab replication close to trivial: the same configuration file produces the same acquisition behavior, and it travels with the data.

The output is where the contract becomes concrete. A session is written into a BIDS layout — `sub-<id>/ses-<n>/` with modality subfolders such as `func/`, `behav/`, and `beh/` — using formats chosen per producer: OME-TIFF (written with the BigTIFF extension, since mesoscope acquisitions routinely exceed the 4-GiB classic-TIFF ceiling) or MP4 for imaging, CSV for serial streams, each accompanied by a per-frame metadata JSON sidecar. Sitting at the session root is a `manifest.json`, an `AcquisitionManifest` from the companion `mesokit-schema` package, which enumerates every producer with its device type, data type, BIDS type, file type, output path, sampling rate, time basis, calibration, and sidecars, together with session identity (subject, session, task, experimenter, protocol) and completion timestamps. The manifest is the contract downstream tools read — they consult it rather than globbing the directory for files that match a naming guess, which the PI summarizes as {no globbing}. **Figure 2** will overlay the three modalities on a shared time axis, mark the primary-device anchor and the synchronization pulse, and show the resulting BIDS tree with the manifest at its root.

## Extensibility: the device contract (Figure 3)

A framework is only modality-agnostic if adding a modality is cheap, so Mesofield reduces "support a new device" to satisfying one small contract. A device class registers itself with the `@DeviceRegistry.register("<type>")` decorator, which maps a YAML `type:` string to the class and stamps that key back onto the class for export; thereafter the `HardwareManager` can materialize the device from any rig file that names it. The convention is that a device's producer (the code that acquires and writes) and its parser (the code that reads the output back during ingest) live in the same file, so the read and write sides of a format cannot silently drift apart. Beyond registration, a device need only expose the standard `DeviceSignals` bundle; everything in the collection and manifest layers then works unchanged, because those layers were written against the signal interface rather than against concrete device types (Figure 1).

The framework ships concrete evidence that this contract is real rather than aspirational. As a worked extension we include a Teensy 4.0 sync pulse generator — a commandable microcontroller running a line-based serial protocol that emits timed LED edges and accepts pattern, run, and synchronization commands — built and used for the synchronization tests. A second extension drives a two-photon rig that combines NI-DAQ TTL signaling with PsychoPy visual stimulation as a subprocess, demonstrating that the same orchestrator spans a different imaging modality and an external stimulus engine. For development and continuous integration, hardware-free mock producers and a dedicated development engine stream synthetic frames with no microcontroller or DAQ present, so the full session — writers, queue, manifest — can be exercised without any physical rig.

The same extension philosophy reaches per-frame computation. A `Procedure` method decorated as a processor, or a `FrameProcessor` constructed and assigned in the subclass, is automatically attached to the named camera, registered with the `DataManager`, and — when requested — plotted live in the GUI. A frame-mean fluorescence readout, for instance, is a few lines that yield a real-time trace during acquisition and a logged data stream on disk, without the author wiring any signals by hand. **Figure 3** will show the single-file device contract (registration, producer, parser) beside the Teensy extension as a concrete instance, and the processor decorator turning a one-line function into an attached, registered, plotted stream.

## Ecosystem: acquisition to analysis (Figure 4)

Because every session is described by a manifest, the analysis side can be written to read the manifest rather than to reverse-engineer a directory. The companion `datakit` toolkit ingests a BIDS session by discovering its manifest and materializing each producer through a source registry that dispatches on the manifest's declared types — the parser half of each device's single-file contract. A `Dataset` is built from a directory and validated cell by cell, with a strict mode that fails loudly on the first malformed stream and a permissive mode that continues and returns a long-format table of errors, so data-integrity problems surface as structured reports rather than as silent gaps. A command-line surface (`build`, `explore`, `profile`, `inspect`, `shell`) exposes these operations, and a `retrofit-manifest` command reconstructs manifests for legacy sessions acquired before the schema existed, so that older data can be brought into the same contract without re-acquisition.

This is also where provenance is meant to extend beyond acquisition. The manifest captures the state at the moment data left the instruments; the intended design carries that description forward through processing stages so that derived outputs — motion-corrected stacks, extracted traces, montages — remain traceable to the raw producers that generated them, feeding into downstream mapping and analysis (for example, [Mesomap]). We flag this as a direction with partial implementation rather than a completed provenance chain; the manifest is the foundation it would build on.

Finally, the framework has been exercised across a deliberately varied set of hardware to test the modality-agnostic claim against reality: a full widefield mesoscope rig, a two-photon rig, a three-camera OpenCV setup, a laptop webcam as a minimal capture device, and both a bespoke wheel and a Janelia-style treadmill as encoders. The quantitative side of this — sustained-acquisition throughput and write speed across camera configurations, behavior under dropped frames and buffer pressure, and the longest continuous run validated — constitutes the Performance and Reliability characterization and is deferred to a supplemental treatment, since reliability claims are only worth what their measurements support. **Figure 4** will trace one session from acquisition through `datakit` ingest to a materialized, validated dataset, with the manifest as the connective tissue at each step.

# Discussion

Mesofield is a wager that the scarce resource in multi-modal mesoscale neuroscience is not device control — which mature tools already provide — but the discipline that connects a reproducible acquisition configuration to a documented, machine-readable output. By routing every session through a single orchestrator, exposing one signal interface that heterogeneous backends satisfy, and emitting a manifest that downstream tools read instead of guessing, the framework aims at three coupled properties: reproducibility, because the same configuration produces the same behavior through the same code path whether driven by GUI or script; shareability, because the configuration and its provenance travel with the data in a standard layout; and {modality-agnostic collection}, because adding a device is a small contract rather than a change to the core. The design's value, if it has one, is less in any single feature than in making the easy thing and the correct thing the same thing — capturing the parameters that generated the data at the moment the data is generated.

We hold the claims deliberately narrow, which is also a way of stating the limits. Mesofield is not a production package; it assumes working knowledge of the hardware and of MicroManager device configuration, and that assumption is a real barrier to a lab without it. It has been developed and exercised primarily on Windows, so portability is plausible but not demonstrated. The reliability characterization — sustained throughput, dropped-frame behavior, synchronization quality across modalities — is exactly the evidence a methods reviewer should demand, and it is the part we mark as forthcoming rather than the part we assert. The provenance chain through processing stages is a design with partial implementation, not a finished pipeline. Most notably, although the project's goal includes Neurodata Without Borders output alongside BIDS, the implemented and validated contract today is BIDS plus the acquisition manifest; NWB export is on the roadmap, and we would rather say so than overstate it. [Other limits: list maximum tested run duration, known synchronization residuals, unsupported device classes once measured.]

Where this goes next follows from those limits. The acquisition manifest is general enough to seed an NWB writer, which would let a single session satisfy both the file-organization standard and the neurophysiology data-language standard from one description. Broadening the set of registered device adapters, hardening synchronization with quantified latency budgets, and extending the manifest cleanly across processing stages would together turn the current acquisition contract into an end-to-end provenance record. None of that requires changing the core abstraction; that the roadmap is mostly additive is itself a modest argument that the central design — one orchestrator, one signal bus, one contract — is carrying the weight it was meant to. [Where next: closed-loop acquisition; multi-rig dataset federation; community device registry.]
